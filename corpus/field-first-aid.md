# Lifeline Field First-Aid Reference

A concise, offline first-aid reference for triage SUPPORT. Educational only — not a
substitute for certified training or professional medical care.

## Severe Bleeding
For life-threatening external bleeding, act immediately. Apply firm, direct pressure on the
wound with a clean cloth or your gloved hand and do not let up. If blood soaks through, add
more material on top rather than removing the soaked layer. Keep pressing. For a bleeding arm
or leg that does not stop with direct pressure, apply a tourniquet a few inches above the wound
(not on a joint), tighten until the bleeding stops, and note the time it was applied. Keep the
person warm and still and get emergency help as fast as possible. Do not remove an embedded
object — press around it instead.

## Choking (Adult or Child Over One Year)
If someone can cough or speak, encourage them to keep coughing — do not interfere. If they
cannot breathe, cough, or speak, act at once. Give up to five firm back blows between the
shoulder blades with the heel of your hand, then up to five abdominal thrusts (stand behind
them, make a fist just above the navel, grasp it with your other hand, and pull sharply inward
and upward). Alternate five back blows and five abdominal thrusts until the object clears or the
person becomes unresponsive. If they become unresponsive, lower them carefully and begin CPR,
checking the mouth for the dislodged object before giving breaths.

## Unresponsive and Not Breathing (CPR)
If a person is unresponsive and not breathing normally, call for emergency help and start CPR.
Place the heel of one hand in the center of the chest, your other hand on top, and push hard and
fast — about two compressions per second, letting the chest fully recoil between compressions.
If trained and willing, give two rescue breaths after every thirty compressions; otherwise give
continuous compressions until help arrives or the person recovers. Use an automated external
defibrillator (AED) as soon as one is available and follow its spoken prompts.

## Burns
Cool the burn immediately with cool (not ice-cold) running water for at least twenty minutes.
Remove jewelry and loose clothing near the burn before swelling begins, but do not peel away
clothing stuck to the skin. Cover the burn loosely with clean, non-stick material such as cling
film or a clean dressing. Do not apply butter, oils, toothpaste, or ice. Seek emergency care for
burns that are large, deep, on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or airway, or that were caused by
chemicals or electricity.

## Heat Stroke
Heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency in which the body overheats and can no longer cool
itself; signs include very hot skin, confusion, agitation, or loss of consciousness. Move the
person to a cool, shaded area at once and call for emergency help. Cool them aggressively: remove
excess clothing and apply cool water to the skin while fanning, or place cool wet cloths or ice
packs at the neck, armpits, and groin. If they are alert and able to swallow, give small sips of
water. Do not give fluids to someone who is confused or not fully conscious.

## Shock
Shock is a dangerous drop in circulation that can follow severe bleeding, burns, or major injury;
signs include pale, cold, clammy skin, rapid breathing, weakness, and confusion. Lay the person
down and, if no spinal or leg injury is suspected, raise their legs slightly. Control any obvious
bleeding, keep them warm with a blanket, loosen tight clothing, and reassure them. Do not give
food or drink. Monitor breathing and be ready to start CPR. Get emergency help urgently.

## Anaphylaxis (Severe Allergic Reaction)
Anaphylaxis is a rapid, life-threatening allergic reaction with signs such as swelling of the face,
lips, tongue or throat, difficulty breathing, widespread hives, and collapse. If the person has an
adrenaline auto-injector (such as an EpiPen), help them use it immediately into the outer thigh,
and call emergency services. A second dose may be given after five to fifteen minutes if symptoms
persist and another injector is available. Help them sit up if breathing is hard, or lie down with
legs raised if they feel faint. Stay with them until help arrives.

## Sprains, Strains, and Suspected Fractures
For a sprain or strain, use the RICE approach: Rest the injury, apply Ice wrapped in cloth for
about twenty minutes at a time, apply gentle Compression with a bandage, and Elevate the limb. For
a suspected broken bone, do not try to straighten it; support and immobilize it in the position
found, padding around it, and seek medical care. Watch for signs of poor circulation beyond the
injury, such as a cold, pale, or numb limb, which needs urgent attention.
